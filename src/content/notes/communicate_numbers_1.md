Techniques to make abstract numbers visceral, memorable, and rooted in human experience.

Source: 
[Heath, C., & Starr, K. (2022). _Making numbers count : the art and science of communicating numbers_. Avid Reader Press.](https://www.amazon.com/Making-Numbers-Count-Science-Communicating/dp/1982165448)

- **Translation:** 
  Never leave numbers in a "foreign language" (math); always provide a phrase that puts them in human terms.
- **Perspective Phrases:** 
  Add brief contextual phrases (e.g., "that’s about the size of 2 Californias") to improve recall accuracy.
- **Avoid Numbers:** 
  The most perfect translations often skip math entirely to deliver concrete, vivid, and meaningful messages.
- **Thought Experiments:** 
  Use simple, concrete analogies—like a gallon jug and ice cubes to explain water scarcity—to help deep truths "click".
- **Familiar Comparisons:** 
  Define new concepts using experiences the audience already knows, such as the feeling of a cross-country flight.
- **The Power of 1:** 
  Focus on a single concrete chunk—1 employee, 1 day, or 1 game—to avoid the confusion of "big-ism".
- **Prototypical Case Studies:** 
  Use stories of a single, representative person or artisan to make large-scale problems like poverty feel actionable.
- **Prototype Scenarios:** 
  Capture reams of demographic data in one unified "prototype" character, like a 32-year-old mom in a grocery store.
- **Favor User-Friendly Numbers:** 
  Use small, whole, and rounded numbers that fit the brain's limited memory capacity.
- **Round with Enthusiasm:** 
  Precision often distracts; use "ballpark" numbers to help the audience grasp the big picture.
- **Concrete Counting:** 
  Always count real things as whole numbers rather than using decimals, fractions, or percentages.
- **Counting in Baskets:**
  Convert small percentages into whole numbers by using a "basket" size (e.g., "1 out of 500").
- **Small & Equal Baskets:** 
  Use the smallest basket possible for "wholeness" (2 in 3) and keep sizes consistent when comparing multiple stats.
- **Defer to Expertise:** If an audience has specialized "chunks" of memory—like baseball fans with batting averages—use their specific clunky formats.
- **Find Your Fathom:** Use units of measurement based on the human body or universal landmarks (e.g., "6 feet" as "1 hockey stick").
- **Simple Multipliers:** Favor multipliers of 1, 2, or half over precise but complex ones like 3.9.
- **The MacGyver Principle:** Look around and build tools from "found objects" or local references known to your specific audience.
- **Target the Counterintuitive:** Use comparisons to highlight surprising disparities, such as comparing the video game industry to the film and music industries.
- **Abstract to Concrete Objects:** Turn units like a "microsecond" into physical objects—like 984 feet of copper wire—to show what is being wasted.
- **Translate to Senses:** Move numbers into the domain of sight, taste, or touch (e.g., comparing tumor sizes to fruit).
- **Vividness:** Create active, colorful, and sensory images that are "close to you," such as imagining the Empire State Building tipped over to block a canal.
- **Personalization:** Frame statistics as a personal threat or opportunity (e.g., "toxins in your own tap water") to wake up the "self" network in the brain.
- **Shrink/Grow to Human Scale:** Bring vast (Mt. Everest) or tiny (nanoparticles) dimensions into the realm of human experience where we are trained to notice things.
- **Interdimensional Recasting:** If a number doesn't make sense, convert it into a different type of quantity—time, space, distance, or even Pringles.
- **Convert to Time:** Use the audience's deep experience with clock and calendar time to explain multipliers (e.g., "a billion seconds is 32 years").
- **Trade Time for Money:** Explain costs by showing how much "life time" is lost to a current inefficient system.
- **Probability as Countable Things:** 
  Express risk in terms of things people can see, like drawing a red "X" on a single word in the million-word _Harry Potter_ series.
- **Calories to Actions:** 
  Translate nutritional counts into well-understood physical actions, like walking two flights of stairs to burn one M&M.
- **Status as a Vertical:** 
  Scaled corpora to physical peaks to represent outlier performance.
- **Magnify Small Quantities:** 
  Blow up tiny achievements (like ant navigation) to human size to build deep respect.
- **Ratios to Life Scenarios:** 
  Magnify ratios to show real-world financial consequences, such as the difference between having $2,000 in savings vs. $20.
- **Accumulate Small Gains:** 
  Add up tiny daily time savings over a year to show they equal massive achievements (e.g., "3 weeks of extra class time").
- **Life Milestones:** 
  Use milestones like a child learning to walk or taking driver's ed to explain long-term durations.
- **Village of 100:** 
  Shrink global demographics to a village of 100 to help people empathize with distributions.
- **Transferred Emotion:** 
  Identify preexisting pools of emotion and use numbers to justify transferring that emotion to your data.
- **Proximal Location Comparisons:** 
  Use local landmarks (like Salisbury Plain) to make a statistic feel active and nearby rather than distant.
- **Emotional symphonies:** 
  Hit multiple harmonious notes (e.g., different infrastructure costs) to build a fuller resonance than one note alone.
- **Incomparables (Summing Competitors):** 
  To show total dominance, add up all competitors and show that your item is still larger than the whole group.
- **Category Jumping:** 
  Move your comparison to a different class entirely, such as comparing a state's economy to a nation's.
- **Mentally Walk the Staircase:** 
  Use an unfolding story where the audience imagines themselves physically moving through the data.
- **Verbing Statistics:** 
  Layer familiar actions on top of concrete nouns so the audience "sees" the data in motion.
- **Sense-Based Demonstrations:** 
  Bring numbers into the room using music, smells, or physical items to create an immersive experience (e.g., the Glove Shrine).
- **Interactive Games:** 
  Use physical actions like clapping to explain sub-second margins in sports.
- **Role-Playing/Substitution:** 
  Pick specific people in the room to represent statistics to give them "skin in the game".
- **Process Over Time:** 
  Avoid "numbing" by spread a big number into recognizable servings (e.g., "1 eBay every 10 days").
- **Bucket Range:** 
  Group steps so they feel "heavy" (like a day’s worth of murders) rather than abstractly small.
- **The Encore:** 
  Deliver a strong impression with part of your figure, then reveal the remaining reserve as a surprising second punch.
- **Crystallize-Break:** 
  First build a pattern of expectations, then shatter it with a surprising "break" (e.g., Apple’s "our thickest is thinner than their thinnest").
- **Orientation Landmarks:** 
  Provide a few key "landmark" numbers to orient people to unfamiliar scales (e.g., hypothermia zones).
- **Existing Maps:** 
  Put data onto familiar structures like a 24-hour day or a calendar year.
- **Flexible Analogies:** 
  Use simple, well-understood systems (like a soccer team) to model complex workplace dynamics.
