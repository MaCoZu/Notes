Selecting the correct medium and formatting visualizations to ensure accuracy and clarity.

Source: [Few, S. (2005). Effectively Communicating Numbers: Selecting the Best Means and Manner of Display, Perceptual Edge.](https://perceptualedge.com/articles/Whitepapers/Communicating_Numbers.pdf)

- **Lookup vs. Pattern Selection:** Use **tables** for precise value lookups and **graphs** to reveal the "shape" of the data (patterns, trends, and exceptions).
- **Relationship Mapping:** Match your data to the specific chart type that represents its underlying relationship:
    - **Time-Series:** Use lines to emphasize shape.
    - **Ranking:** Sequence by size, usually with bars.
    - **Part-to-Whole:** Use bars; **avoid pie charts** because area is harder to compare than length.
    - **Deviation:** Use lines or points to show differences from a target.
    - **Distribution:** Use histograms (vertical bars) or frequency polygons (lines).
    - **Correlation:** Use scatter plots with points and trend lines.
    - **Nominal Comparison:** Use bars.
- **Attribute Power Hierarchy:** Prioritize **2-D position** and **line length** as the most powerful visual attributes for encoding values.
- **Zero-Baseline Requirement:** Bar graphs **must start at zero** to prevent misleading the viewer regarding the magnitude of differences.
- **Switching Bars to Points:** If you must "zoom in" on a narrow range that does not include zero, switch from bars to points to maintain accuracy.
- **Small Multiples:** Series of small, identical graphs arranged together to display a fourth variable without using cluttered 3-D.
- **Proximity for Variables:** Position variables you want to compare directly next to each other in the layout.
- **Horizontal Orientation:** Use horizontal bars when labels are long or there are many items to prevent squeezed text.
- **Scale Buffering:** Adjust the quantitative scale to extend slightly below the lowest and above the highest value.
- **Direct Labeling:** Label lines directly at their endpoints instead of using a distant legend.
- **Legend Matching:** Arrange legend labels in the same order as the data bars/lines they represent.
- **Signal-to-Noise/Distraction Removal:** Remove anything that does not essential to the meaning, such as background images or unnecessary grid lines.
- **Visual Muting:** Render supporting elements (axes, grid lines) in **light gray** to make the data stand out.
- **Soft Color Palettes:** Use soft, natural colors for the majority of the data; reserve **bright or saturated colors** for highlighting important points.
- **Highlighting Techniques:** Make specific data stand out using contrasting borders, thicker lines, or larger point sizes.
- **Tick Mark Limitation:** Use only 5 to 10 major tick marks on an axis to avoid clutter.
- **Dual Scales:** On very large graphs, place scales on both sides to help the eye identify values accurately.
- **Zero-Position Axes:** For scales involving positive and negative numbers, position the axis line at zero.
- **Descriptive Notes:** Include notes directly on the graph to describe specific events or provide instructions on how to interpret complex views.
- **Series Hiding:** Use software features to hide distracting series in a chart while keeping the data accessible in a supporting table.
- **Slicers:** Display additional variables by allowing users to filter through them interactively.
